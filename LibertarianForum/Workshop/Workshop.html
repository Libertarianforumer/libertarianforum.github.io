INTRO

Libertarianism has a long history, being philosophically deep-rooted in the ancient times. However, as a clearly defined ideological worldview, it has emerged on the brink of the XIX and XX centuries based on the classical version of liberalism, the principles of which were put forward by Adam Smith and Alexis de Tocqueville. Originally, liberals were standing for many ideals shared by contemporary libertarians: civil liberties, rule of law, and private property, opposing absolute monarchy, dictatorship, and slavery. However, given that many of its members have at some point raised the banner of statism and adopted the interventionist policies, the liberal camp fell apart, with those loyal to the ideas of individualism and free market forming their own political movement.

The foundations of libertarianism were laid by such notable philosophers and economists as Milton Friedman, Friedrich Hayek, James M. Buchanan, Ayn Rand, Murray Rothbard, and many others. Whilst socio-political context was largely defined by Buchanan's, Rothbard's and Rand's works, the economic framework is based on two powerful schools of thought, namely Austrian School and Chicago School, to which Hayek and Friedman belonged.
